Which Rung Are You On?

A self-assessment for Utah voters

The Moment I Realized I Wasn't Thinking

Let me tell you about the moment I caught myself being an idiot.

It was February 2025. HB267‚Äîthe collective bargaining bill‚Äîjust passed. My immediate reaction? Rage. How could they ban unions for teachers and firefighters? I pulled up Twitter, ready to blast the bill's sponsors.

Then I stopped. A question popped into my head: "Have you actually read HB267?"

No. I hadn't.

I'd seen headlines. I'd seen organizations I follow condemning it. I'd seen my party's talking points. But I hadn't read the actual bill. I was reacting to who supported it, not what it said.

This was the moment I realized: I wasn't thinking. I was tribally reacting.

And if I'm being honest with myself‚Äîwhich is the whole point of this essay‚ÄîI do this all the time. You probably do too. We all do. The question is: do we want to keep doing it?

The Political Ladder: Where Do You Stand?

Tim Urban, the writer behind Wait But Why, has a framework that changed how I see political arguments. He describes political thinking as a ladder with different rungs. Most of us spend most of our time on the bottom rungs, even though we think we're up high.

Here's how it works:

The Three Levels

üî• THE ZEALOT (Bottom Rung)

  • Mindset: "My side is right, your side is evil/stupid/corrupt"
  • Behavior: Doesn't question their own beliefs. Ever.
  • When challenged: Gets angry, attacks the person, dismisses without engaging
  • Motto: "If you don't agree with me, you're the problem"

Think of the person who calls everyone who disagrees a "communist" or a "fascist." They're not thinking‚Äîthey're waging tribal war.

‚öΩ THE SPORTS FAN (Middle Rung)

  • Mindset: "My team vs your team"
  • Behavior: Roots for their side to win, doesn't really care about the issues
  • When challenged: Deflects, whataboutism, moves goalposts
  • Motto: "My party, right or wrong"

The Sports Fan knows politics is more nuanced than the Zealot thinks, but they've still picked a team and they're sticking with it. They might admit their side makes mistakes, but they'll still vote straight ticket and cheer when "their team" wins.

üß† THE THINKER (High Rung)

  • Mindset: "What's actually true? What actually works?"
  • Behavior: Questions their own beliefs, genuinely curious about opposing views
  • When challenged: Listens, asks questions, changes mind when evidence warrants
  • Motto: "I could be wrong. Let's find out together."

The Thinker has principles, but they're willing to be persuaded. They vote based on issues, not teams. They can explain opposing views without strawmanning them. They're uncomfortable with certainty.

The Uncomfortable Truth

Here's what Tim Urban points out: we all think we're Thinkers.

Ask anyone what rung they're on, and they'll say High Rung. "I'm reasonable! I consider all sides! I'm not tribal like those people."

But here's the test: When was the last time you changed your mind about a major political belief?

If your answer is "never" or "not in the last 5 years," you're probably not on the High Rung as much as you think.

High-Rung thinkers change their minds regularly. Not on everything‚Äîthey have principles‚Äîbut on specific policies, specific candidates, specific approaches. Because they're following the evidence, not the tribe.

How This Shows Up in Utah Politics

Let's bring this home. You're using this Utah Legislative Tracker to follow bills. That's great! You're engaged. But how are you engaging?

Low-Rung Questions:

  • "Which party sponsored this bill?" (checking which team to root for)
  • "Do the organizations I follow support it?" (tribal loyalty test)
  • "Will this make my political enemies mad?" (Sports Fan thinking)

High-Rung Questions:

  • "What problem is this bill trying to solve?"
  • "What are the strongest arguments FOR this bill?"
  • "What are the strongest arguments AGAINST it?"
  • "What would change my mind on this issue?"

Let's take a real example: School Vouchers

Low-Rung Liberal: "School vouchers defund public schools! Republicans just want to destroy public education because they hate teachers!"

Low-Rung Conservative: "Public schools are indoctrination camps! Anyone who opposes vouchers just wants to protect the failing status quo!"

High-Rung Thinking: "Vouchers give low-income families options that wealthy families already have through private schools or moving to better districts. But vouchers also divert funding from public schools that serve 95% of students, and early evidence from Milwaukee and Cleveland shows mixed academic results. The question is: do we prioritize choice or universal quality? What's the right balance? What does the data actually show?"

See the difference? High-Rung thinking doesn't mean you don't have an opinion. It means you can articulate why the other side disagrees without calling them evil or stupid.
